Research Findings

The Hidden Cost
of Being Female

Regression-adjusted analysis of a longitudinal labor panel reveals a persistent gender wage penalty, a compounding effect from salary history disclosure, and an inconclusive early signal from California's 2018 ban — raising questions about whether structural remedies can outpace entrenched compensation anchors.

By LMU Datathon Team  ·  Panel Dataset Analysis  ·  Western U.S. Labor Market

88% Wage premium in the highest-gap industry (Mining)
0.55 Prior pay coefficient post CA ban — up from 0.50

This analysis draws on a longitudinal panel dataset provided by LMU, in which individuals are observed across multiple time periods alongside their occupation, hours worked, demographic characteristics, and recorded wages. The panel structure is analytically significant: it enables within-person comparisons over time, reducing the risk that cross-sectional confounders — differences in industry selection, educational attainment, or regional labor markets — are driving observed wage differentials. Three research questions guided the inquiry: whether a measurable gender wage gap exists after covariate adjustment, whether employer salary disclosure requirements amplify that gap, and whether California's 2018 prohibition on prior salary inquiries has altered the prior-pay-to-current-pay transmission mechanism.

Across all three questions, the data points in a consistent and concerning direction — but also reveals the limits of what the current evidence window can establish.

Finding 01

A regression-adjusted 10% gender penalty survives full covariate control

The most direct test of gender-based wage discrimination is a multivariate regression that isolates the female wage coefficient after controlling for all observable productivity-related factors. When education, work experience, weekly hours, occupation group, and industry are held constant, the model yields a female wage coefficient of approximately −0.10 — meaning female employees earn roughly 10% less than male employees in otherwise identical positions. This estimate is not a raw descriptive gap, which can be inflated by occupational sorting; it is the residual penalty that persists within the same job categories and skill levels.

Descriptively, the median hourly wage for women in the dataset was $9.30 versus $10.00 for men. The distribution, however, is more revealing than the medians alone. The right-skew in male wages — where the upper tail extends considerably further — indicates that the gap is not uniform. It narrows near the lower end of the distribution and widens substantially at higher wage levels, consistent with a "glass ceiling" dynamic in which high-earning roles disproportionately reward male incumbents beyond what observable qualifications would predict.

Finding 02

Salary history disclosure operates as a gap-amplification mechanism

The 10% adjusted penalty identified in Finding 01 assumes a labor market where wage offers are set independently of prior earnings. That assumption does not hold uniformly. To test whether salary history disclosure exacerbates the gender gap, occupation group was used as a proxy variable: white-collar roles were classified as sectors that routinely request prior pay; blue-collar roles were classified as those that generally do not. While this operationalization is an approximation — not all white-collar employers ask, nor all blue-collar ones stay silent — it provides a tractable first-order test of the disclosure mechanism.

The results are directionally clear. In the no-disclosure group, the absolute wage gap is $1.50/hr (female: $9.30; male: $10.80). In the disclosure group, the gap nearly doubles in absolute terms to $3.40/hr (female: $14.50; male: $17.90). It is important to note that the higher absolute levels in the disclosure group partly reflect the higher-earning nature of white-collar work; the relevant signal is the widened differential, not the absolute wages. This differential widening is consistent with the anchoring hypothesis: if historical female wages already encode prior discrimination, then using them as a bargaining anchor in a new negotiation imports that discrimination forward, compounding it across job transitions rather than correcting it.

A key analytical caution applies here: the occupation-group proxy introduces selection bias. Women who sort into white-collar jobs may differ systematically from those in blue-collar roles in ways the model does not fully capture — differences in negotiation behavior, network access, or informal wage-setting norms. The disclosure mechanism therefore cannot be declared causal from this analysis alone. What the data does establish is a strong conditional association: the gender gap is substantially larger in the segment of the labor market where prior pay information is most likely to influence the negotiation.

Finding 03

Post-ban coefficient provides an inconclusive but theoretically instructive signal

California's January 2018 salary history ban created a natural experiment: a discrete policy intervention that, in principle, should reduce the weight employers place on prior pay when setting new wages. The evaluation approach here is a rolling annual regression of current wage on prior wage within the Western U.S. region, which allows the prior-pay coefficient to be tracked year-by-year as an indicator of how strongly historical earnings transmit into new compensation offers.

Contrary to the policy's intended effect, the coefficient did not decline post-ban. The pre-2018 mean coefficient was approximately 0.4975; the post-2018 estimate rose to 0.5542. Before interpreting this as evidence of policy failure, however, several confounds must be considered. First, the post-ban observation window covers only 3–4 years of data, which is insufficient to distinguish a structural shift from year-to-year noise given the pre-ban coefficient's own volatility (ranging from 0.07 to 0.54 across the pre-period). Second, the coefficient will never approach zero even under a fully effective ban, because prior pay legitimately proxies for skills and accumulated human capital that are independently wage-relevant. The meaningful evaluation question is not whether the coefficient equals zero, but whether it declined relative to a counterfactual non-ban trajectory — a test that requires more post-period data than is currently available.

What the post-ban period does reveal, beyond the uptick in the point estimate, is a marked reduction in coefficient volatility. Pre-2018, the annual coefficient ranged widely from 0.07 to 0.54 — a spread that reflects genuine year-to-year instability in how strongly prior pay transmitted into wages, potentially driven by business cycle variation, shifting labor market tightness, or inconsistent employer practices. Post-2018, the coefficient clusters tightly in the 0.50–0.55 range. This convergence suggests that while the ban has not weakened the prior pay relationship, it may have stabilized it — possibly by removing the most opportunistic uses of salary history (the years with very low coefficients) while leaving the structurally embedded transmission intact. In other words, the floor of the relationship has risen even as the ceiling has not fallen. The lingering effect is not just persistent; it is now more reliably so.

One interpretation is that compliance and enforcement are lagging the legislation. Employers who previously used salary history openly may now be capturing the same information through informal channels — referrals, background checks, or inferred ranges from job title history — rendering the formal prohibition partially porous and producing a more uniform, if still inequitable, baseline. Distinguishing between these explanations would require a longer post-ban data series and ideally a difference-in-differences design comparing California against a matched non-ban state, which would allow the policy's counterfactual trajectory to be estimated rather than assumed.

Finding 04

Industry-level wage premiums reveal where structural factors most amplify inequality

The aggregate 10% gender penalty masks substantial heterogeneity across the economy. To decompose this, the log-wage model was extended with industry fixed effects, yielding regression coefficients that represent each sector's wage premium above a common reference baseline, controlling for the individual-level factors already described. These premiums capture industry-level wage-setting norms, skill scarcity, unionization rates, and physical risk compensation — all independent of who the worker is.

The practical implication for gender equity is this: the larger a sector's wage premium, the more the absolute dollar value of any proportional gender gap within that sector. A 10% penalty on a $10/hr wage is $1.00. The same proportional penalty on a $17.90/hr white-collar wage — inflated partly by industry premiums — is $1.79. The penalty rate may be constant; its economic magnitude is not. This multiplicative dynamic means that female workers who manage to enter high-premium industries like Finance and Insurance (51% premium) still face a larger absolute earnings loss from the gender penalty than women in lower-premium sectors. Sector entry is a necessary but insufficient condition for closing the gap.

Mining's 88% premium is the most extreme in the dataset and reflects a confluence of hazard pay, remote location premiums, and a workforce that is over 80% male nationally — meaning the sector premium and the gender composition problem are correlated rather than independent. Construction (56%) and Utilities (55%) share similar characteristics. The more analytically interesting case may be Finance and Insurance (51%), where the premium reflects cognitive skill scarcity and organizational compensation norms rather than physical risk. This sector is also heavily implicated in the salary-history disclosure finding, creating a situation where two amplifying mechanisms — industry premium and prior pay anchoring — operate simultaneously on the same population of workers.

Analytical Summary

  • Adjusted gender penalty of ~10% survives full covariate control across education, experience, hours, occupation, and industry — ruling out compositional explanations
  • Salary history proxy analysis shows the absolute gender gap nearly doubles ($1.50 → $3.40/hr) in disclosure-associated sectors, consistent with prior-pay anchoring theory; causal identification limited by occupational selection bias
  • Post-ban prior pay coefficient (0.55) exceeds pre-ban mean (0.50), but the short observation window and pre-period volatility preclude any confident conclusion about policy efficacy — a difference-in-differences design with a control state would strengthen inference
  • Industry premiums (39–88%) amplify the absolute dollar cost of the gender penalty multiplicatively; high-premium sectors like Finance & Insurance concentrate both the disclosure mechanism and the industry premium simultaneously
  • Data limitation: unmeasured variables including negotiation behavior, informal network access, and firm-level compensation policies remain potential confounders that the panel model cannot fully absorb

Taken together, these findings suggest that wage inequality in this dataset is not attributable to a single mechanism but to a set of reinforcing structural processes: a baseline penalty that survives individual-level controls; a hiring practice that compounds historical inequity at each job transition; high-premium industries that multiply the absolute cost of any proportional gap; and a legislative intervention whose effectiveness cannot yet be established. The policy implication is that single-point interventions — a salary ban alone, for instance — are unlikely to be sufficient if the other mechanisms remain intact. Addressing the gap structurally would require coordinated action across hiring practices, industry-level compensation norms, and enforcement infrastructure, alongside longer data collection to measure what is and is not working.
